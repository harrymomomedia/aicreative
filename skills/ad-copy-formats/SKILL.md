# Ad Copy Formats (primary text)

## Overview

The #1 failure mode when writing a batch of lead-gen primaries: **every ad uses the same skeleton** —
`hook → "if a staff member sexually abused you… you may qualify" → ✅✅✅ feature bullets → "👉 Tap to see if you qualify"`.
Even with different angles, that one *shape* repeated 20–30× reads as templated and fatigues the feed.

**Core principle: vary the copy FORMAT across the batch, not just the angle.** This skill is the menu of
12 distinct primary-text structures + one canonical example of each. Pull a different format for each
ad so a set of 30 never reads the same twice.

**Mix rules for a batch:**
- **~30% long-form (500+ words)** — formats #2 (first-person advertorial) and #3 (journalistic exposé), plus long #4 letters. These convert well in tort lead-gen.
- **Classic DR (#12) used SPARINGLY** — only 3–4 in a large batch; it's the skeleton everything else is escaping.
- **No single format used more than ~3×** in a set; **no headline more than ~2–3×**.
- Pair the format to the creative: redacted-document image → exposé; postcard image → open letter; Q&A image → FAQ; tap-grid image → micro; portrait → first-person.

## The 12-format menu

| # | Format | Shape | Typical length | Best paired with |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Micro** | 2–3 lines, no structure | ~25–40 w | tap-grids, ID card, fast-UI |
| 2 | **First-person advertorial** | story, no bullets | **500+ w** | testimonial portraits, then/now |
| 3 | **Journalistic exposé** | 3rd-person report | **500+ w** | redacted grievance, news clipping |
| 4 | **Open letter** | "To the woman reading this…" | med–long | postcard, quiet portrait |
| 5 | **FAQ / Q&A** | real questions answered | medium | Q&A/AMA image |
| 6 | **Myth vs. Fact** | MYTH/FACT pairs | short–med | reframe, "no paperwork" |
| 7 | **Listicle** | "3 things you should know" | medium | checklist, facility cards |
| 8 | **Timeline** | "2024… 2025… now…" | medium | settlement-precedent, news |
| 9 | **Pure prose** | emotional, no bullets/CTA-stack | short–med | atmospheric, "feeling is enough" |
| 10 | **Checklist-led** | opens ON the ✅ qualifiers | short | facility grid, eligibility |
| 11 | **Dialogue / overheard** | two voices | short–med | iMessage, two-women |
| 12 | **Classic DR** | hook + qualify + ✅ + CTA | short–med | use sparingly (3–4/batch) |
| 13 | **PAS** | Problem → Agitate → Solution | medium | emotional portraits, prose images |
| 14 | **"Did you know?" fact-hook** | one true fact → pivot | short–med | reframe, educational |
| 15 | **Second-person mini-story** | "You were 22…" reader as protagonist | medium | portraits, atmospheric |
| 16 | **Conditional cascade** | "If you… If you…" build to yes | short–med | facility/eligibility, checklist |
| 17 | **Objection-cascade** | "You're probably thinking…" answer each | short–med | reframe, "no paperwork" |
| 18 | **Social-proof quote stack** | 3 short illustrative survivor lines | short–med | quote card, survivors-counter |

**Devices (apply on top of ANY format):**
- **P.S. close** — end a letter/advertorial with "P.S. — it's free, and no one ever has to know you looked." (high-converting DR device)
- **Open-loop hook** — first line opens a curiosity gap ("Most women who were inside don't realize this one thing…"), resolved later in the copy.

## Locked compliance (apply to EVERY primary, every format)

- **Recovery phrasing:** "**significant potential compensation**" + "**may qualify**". NEVER "owed", "guaranteed", "settlement", "paid", "entitled to compensation", or "win" about the *viewer's* recovery. (IL JDC exception: "significant compensation" — drop "potential" — is OK there; women's-prison keeps the full phrase.)
- **Facility list** where natural: Chowchilla (CCWF), Valley State Prison, California Institution for Women (Chino/CIW), Folsom Women's Facility.
- **Feature/objection stack** to draw from: no police report or proof needed · 100% confidential · free, no upfront cost (you never pay unless compensation is recovered) · even if it was years ago · even if you never reported it · you may qualify even if you can only name or describe the officer.
- **Privacy, NOT no-contact:** lawyers DO reach out — the promise is confidentiality. Never "nobody calls you / shows up." Say "confidential," "nobody in your life has to know."
- **Long-forms must include** "no outcome is guaranteed; every case is different."
- **Numbers = factual past events only** (FCI Dublin 2024 ≈ $1M+ each; a former guard convicted). No invented per-person figures; "what could your case be worth?" must show NO dollar amount.
- **Disclaimer:** append the verbatim Pulaski/Jones text (skill `pulaski-jones-disclaimer`) to the copy row, and/or burn it on the image (`scripts/burn_disclaimer_image.py --style bar`).
- **Say "sexual abuse" explicitly**; "sexually abused by a staff member" is the standard construction.

## The 12 examples (canonical reference)

### 1 — Micro
> No report. No proof. No paperwork. You can still qualify. Sexually abused by staff at a California women's prison? Free, confidential, about 2 minutes. 👉 See if you qualify.

### 2 — First-person advertorial (≈480 words; each one a DIFFERENT woman/voice/facility so they never repeat)
> My daughter is the one who found it. She's 26 now — older than I was when I went in.
>
> I did three years at CIW in Chino. I was 23, scared, and I learned fast that the quickest way to get written up, moved, or have your visits messed with was to be a "problem." So when one of the officers started finding reasons to be alone with me, I told myself it was the price of getting through. He'd say things. Then it was more than things. And every time, the message underneath was the same: who do you think they're going to believe?
>
> I never told anyone. Not the counselor — she worked with him. Not my mom. Not my daughter, ever. I got out, got a job, built a life around a locked door in my own head, and called it healing. It wasn't. It was just quiet.
>
> A couple weeks ago my daughter sent me a link — she didn't know, she just saw something about women from California's prisons. "Mom, isn't this where you were?" I almost didn't open it.
>
> What it said: women sexually abused by staff inside California's women's prisons may qualify for significant potential compensation. Not just the federal one everybody heard about — the state prisons too. Chino. Chowchilla. Valley State. Folsom.
>
> I read it three times looking for the catch. Here's what I found instead, and I'm telling you because I wish someone had told me twenty years ago:
>
> You don't need a police report — I never made one. It doesn't matter. You don't need proof, records, or even his full name; if you can describe him, that can be enough. It's free — you never pay out of pocket unless compensation is actually recovered. And it's private: no one called my house, no one showed up, my daughter still only knows what I choose to tell her.
>
> It took maybe two minutes. Two minutes, against twenty years of carrying it alone.
>
> I don't know how my case turns out — nobody can promise that. But I know what it felt like to finally say the true thing out loud and have someone treat it like it mattered. Like I mattered.
>
> If your stomach dropped reading this because you know exactly what I mean — you're not the only one, and it is not too late. I thought it was. I was wrong. 👉 Tap below to see, privately, if you qualify.

### 3 — Journalistic exposé (≈480 words)
> For nearly two decades, women inside California's prisons reported a version of the same story.
>
> A correctional officer who found reasons to be alone with them. A counselor who traded "favors" for phone time, commissary, or a clean disciplinary record. A night-shift staffer who knew the cameras didn't reach certain corners — and that an inmate's word rarely outweighs a badge.
>
> The women who tried to report it often watched their complaints disappear. Grievances dismissed. Some say they were moved, written up, or sent to solitary for speaking at all — punished while the staff member stayed on the job. The message, repeated until it was a kind of law inside those walls: no one will believe you, so don't bother.
>
> For a long time, that held. It isn't holding anymore.
>
> Across the state, survivors are coming forward about sexual abuse by staff in California's women's prisons — Central California Women's Facility (Chowchilla/CCWF), Valley State Prison, the California Institution for Women (Chino/CIW), and Folsom Women's Facility. Attorneys are reviewing claims, and women who were abused may qualify for significant potential compensation.
>
> What's striking is how many believed they had no case. Most never filed a police report. Most have no documentation. Many never told a single person. None of that is disqualifying.
>
> A survivor doesn't need a police report or any physical proof. She may qualify even if she remembers only the officer's face, not his name. It doesn't matter how many years have passed, or that she stayed silent. The review is free — nothing out of pocket, and a fee only if compensation is recovered. And it's confidential: the people in her life aren't contacted, and nothing is shared without her say.
>
> The hardest part is also the shortest — a brief, private form, about two minutes. For women certain that what happened would never be acknowledged, that's often the first time anyone treated it as real.
>
> No outcome is guaranteed; every case is different. But the door that was closed for so long is, for the first time, open.
>
> If you were sexually abused by a staff member while incarcerated in a California women's prison, you may qualify for significant potential compensation. 👉 Tap below for a free, confidential review.

### 4 — Open letter
> To the woman reading this who already knows what it's about —
> You don't have to say it out loud. You don't need the right words, or any words. I just need you to know a few things nobody told you back then.
> What a staff member did to you inside was not your fault — not the uniform, not anything you said, not the fact that you didn't fight or didn't tell. In California, a guard cannot have your consent while you're in their custody. It was abuse. Full stop.
> And it's not too late. Women from Chowchilla, Valley State, CIW, and Folsom may qualify for significant potential compensation for exactly this. No proof. No report. No one in your life has to find out.
> If you've read this far, that's your answer about whether to look into it.
> 👉 Whenever you're ready — free, private, about two minutes.

### 5 — FAQ / Q&A
> A few real questions we get:
> **"Do I need proof or a police report?"** No.
> **"It was 15 years ago — too late?"** Usually not.
> **"Will my family find out?"** No. It's confidential.
> **"What if I only remember his face, not his name?"** That can be enough.
> If a staff member sexually abused you at Chowchilla, Valley State, CIW, or Folsom, you may qualify for significant potential compensation. 👉 Tap to ask your own question, privately.

### 6 — Myth vs. Fact
> **MYTH:** No one believes an inmate over a guard. **FACT:** California law is on your side now.
> **MYTH:** It was too long ago. **FACT:** Many survivors still qualify, years later.
> **MYTH:** I never reported it, so I can't. **FACT:** You don't need a report — or any proof.
> Sexually abused by staff in a CA women's prison? You may qualify for significant potential compensation. 👉 Tap to see.

> 1. If a staff member sexually abused you, it was a crime — not "just how it was." Someone in custody can't consent to a guard.

### 8 — Timeline

> → 2025: A former California prison guard was convicted of sexually abusing women in his custody.
> → Now: Survivors from state women's prisons — Chowchilla, Valley State, CIW, Folsom — are coming forward, and may qualify for significant potential compensation.

Years later, the quiet is still there. But the part where speaking up costs you everything and changes nothing? That part isn't true anymore. ## Headlines

Rotate a pool of ~10 short headlines; no headline more than ~2–3× in a set. Mix question hooks
("Sexually Abused in a California Women's Prison?", "Were You at Chowchilla, CIW, or Valley State?"),
reassurance ("Check If You Qualify — Free & Confidential"), and emotional/urgency ("It's Not Too
Late to Come Forward", "Do You Remember What They Did?"). Keep facility names + "may qualify" feel.

## Adapting to other campaigns

The 12 formats are campaign-agnostic. For IL JDC swap the facility list (Audy Home/Cook County JTDC,
IYC St. Charles/Harrisburg/Kewanee), keep child-safety split rules (never "kid/juvenile" + "sexually
abused" in the same sentence/clip per `admachin-video-ads`), and use "significant compensation" (IL
exception). The structure menu and mix rules carry over unchanged.
